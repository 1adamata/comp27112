Why this chapter matters

A teapot that lost its shape

So far in the pipeline we have modelled geometry, projected it, rasterised it and even mapped textures onto it. We quietly assumed the renderer would "figure out" how light plays across a surface. It does not. With everything we have built so far, our shiny ceramic teapot renders as something disturbingly flat — all sense of depth, curvature and material simply gone.

The missing ingredient is a model of light and how it interacts with materials. Without it, any scene — and arguably the real world — looks dull and flat, or is invisible entirely. This chapter builds that model from first principles, one term at a time, until we arrive at the equation that drives real-time graphics.

Section 01 · The physics

How surfaces reflect light

Return to the original idea: rays of light (let's keep it simple and say white) leave a source and strike objects in the scene. Rays that bounce away from the eye are never seen — our eyes cannot recover that energy. The interesting rays are the ones that do reach the eye. What happens to them is dictated entirely by the material the surface is made of.

Diffuse reflection — light scatters everywhere

The most obvious material property is colour. If a surface is grey, every part of the white spectrum that isn't grey is absorbed, and the greyish component is reflected back. Crucially, on a matte surface this reflected light scatters uniformly in all directions. Trace the shape of that scattering and you get a near-perfect hemisphere (a semicircle in 2D). This is diffuse reflection: think felt, sponges, carpets, bricks — surfaces that look the same brightness no matter where you stand.

Why this matters

If we know the shape of the scattering, we can deduce the material. Real devices scan how light scatters off physical materials, and those profiles are used to build virtual materials — essential for simulation, architecture, and photorealistic games.

Specular reflection — a preferred direction

Do the same experiment with the shiny teapot and something changes: the light still scatters, but it is clearly stronger in one direction. You see bright whitish reflections from certain patches of the surface. These are specular reflections — points where light shoots out with more energy. The scattering profile shows a distinct bump toward the direction of maximum reflection.

The perfect mirror — the limiting case

Push specularity to the extreme and you reach a perfect mirror: all incident light bounces off at the perfect reflection angle (mirrored about the surface normal), exactly as in a physics class. Every incoming ray reflects at one precise angle.

Imperfect specular — the real world

Most materials are not perfect mirrors (thankfully). They show imperfect specular reflection: some parts reflect strongly, but there is scattering in other directions too, creating that dome-shaped profile. The cause is microscopic roughness — zoom in and the surface is never perfectly smooth, which scatters the rays. Ceramics and metals behave this way.

Most materials are both

Materials aren't binary — they're rarely purely shiny or purely diffuse. Real surfaces combine both. So we combine the two scattering profiles, and that combination is the material. From here on we work with exactly two ingredients: a diffuse component and a specular component (if any).

Section 02 · The flat teapot, explained

Why it looked flat: ambient light

So why did our teapot render flat? In every material example above, we needed the direction the light comes from. But before this chapter we never had light directions — we assumed a single uniform light spread evenly across the whole scene. That uniform light is called ambient light.

The stronger the ambient light, the more of the teapot we see — but because it is identical everywhere, we have no information about how light would actually bounce. So the model's appearance depends only on how strong the ambient term is, normalised here between 0 and 1.

Ambient-only shading

Ambient light has no direction. Slide it and watch the teapot get brighter — but never gain any shape. This is precisely the problem.

Section 03 · Modelling a light source

Lambert's cosine law

Picture a simple Blender scene: a flat plane and a light source. The plane has a surface normal \(\hat{\mathbf N}\). We placed the light, so we know where it is — meaning we can define a vector \(\hat{\mathbf L}\) pointing toward it. That gives us the light direction. The light also has an intensity \(I\) shining on the surface.

Keep it 2D. Take a patch of surface of width \(d = 1\) unit. With the light directly overhead, the light per unit equals \(I\) (since \(d=1\)). Now rotate the light around a point on the plane by angle \(\theta\). The incoming beam now strikes at a slant: the same beam must cover a larger width \(d'\!>d\), so each unit of surface receives less light. The surface gets darker.

The original beam width \(d\) is still perpendicular to the light direction, so sliding it down to the surface creates a right triangle. The angle between \(d\) and \(d'\) is also \(\theta\). Since it's a right triangle and \(d=1\):

$$\cos\theta = \frac{1}{d'} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad I_d = \frac{I}{d'} = I\cos\theta$$

This is the key insight: the intensity landing on the surface scales with the cosine of the angle between the light direction and the surface normal. At \(\theta = 0\) the light is straight on, \(\cos 0 = 1\), and we get full intensity. As the angle grows, intensity falls.

And because \(\hat{\mathbf N}\) and \(\hat{\mathbf L}\) are unit vectors, \(\cos\theta\) is simply their dot product:

$$I_d = I\cos\theta = I\,(\hat{\mathbf N}\cdot\hat{\mathbf L})$$
Works both ways

The relationship holds whether you move the light or tilt the surface — the normal moves with the plane. Tilt the surface so the normal faces away and \(\cos\theta\) goes negative. We can't have negative light, so we clamp it: \(\max(0,\ \hat{\mathbf N}\cdot\hat{\mathbf L})\). (Solve negative light and you'll win a Nobel Prize in quantum physics.)

Section 04 · The diffuse term

Giving the teapot its colour back

Now put the teapot back and give it a colour, written \(k_d\) (the diffuse reflection coefficient). Move the light around and the teapot's shading follows Lambert's law exactly. To get the correct colour at a point, multiply the material colour by the light model:

$$I_d = I\,k_d\cos\theta = I\,k_d\,(\hat{\mathbf N}\cdot\hat{\mathbf L})$$
\(k_d\) can be a texture

\(k_d\) doesn't have to be a flat colour. Since we can sample textures and map them to models, we simply look up \(k_d\) at the surface point after texture mapping and multiply it by the light model. Everything we built earlier in the pipeline plugs straight in.

We've gone from an awful flat render to something that reads as a real shape — we can see the shadowing and understand the form. But a real ceramic teapot is shiny, and this one looks completely matte. We need to add highlights.

Section 05 · The specular term

Adding the shine

Watch the shiny teapot: as the light moves, the white highlights move too. But unlike diffuse shading, the highlight also depends on where you are looking from — think of the glare off a metal surface that hits your eye at just the right angle. (It's exactly why lecture theatres avoid reflective surfaces — projector light would bounce into someone's face.)

So specular reflection depends on both the light position and the viewing position. We bring in the camera. Simplify back to a 2D plane with specular properties. We now have four players:

N̂ surface normal L̂ to light R̂ perfect reflection of L V̂ to viewer

We only care about the specular component, so we use the perfect reflection ray \(\hat{\mathbf R}\). The highlight is defined by the angle \(\phi\) between \(\hat{\mathbf R}\) and the viewing direction \(\hat{\mathbf V}\). As before, the cosine of that angle is a dot product:

$$I_s = \hat{\mathbf R}\cdot\hat{\mathbf V} = \cos\phi$$

The problem: the whole teapot lights up

Add that raw term and it's wrong — almost the entire teapot becomes a highlight. Why? When \(\hat{\mathbf V}\) aligns with \(\hat{\mathbf R}\), the dot product is 1, as expected. But move slightly off-axis and we want a sharp drop-off — yet the cosine is still large (say 0.8), meaning 80% of the surface still reads as highlight. Not what a shiny material does.

The fix: raise to a power

The trick is to raise the dot product to a power \(n\). Since \(\cos\phi\) lies between −1 and 1, raising it to a power shrinks the highlight: off-axis values collapse toward zero much faster, leaving a small bright spot.

$$I_s = k_s\,(\hat{\mathbf R}\cdot\hat{\mathbf V})^{\,n} = k_s\cos^{\,n}\!\phi$$

Here \(k_s\) is the specular colour — usually white, though some metals tint it. This whole expression is the specular reflection term. The exponent \(n\) (the shininess) controls highlight size: bigger \(n\) → tighter, smaller, more circular highlight. As \(n\to\infty\) you approach a perfect mirror — a single ray reflecting from a vanishingly small point.

Section 06 · The geometry

Where does R̂ come from?

We used \(\hat{\mathbf R}\), the perfect reflection of \(\hat{\mathbf L}\) about the normal — but we still have to compute it. Here's the derivation, using only vector projection from the very first maths lecture.

Define \(\mathbf N'\) as the projection of \(\hat{\mathbf L}\) onto the normal \(\hat{\mathbf N}\). The line joining \(\hat{\mathbf L}\) and \(\hat{\mathbf R}\) crosses the normal at a right angle, so:

$$\mathbf N' = \text{proj}_{L\to N} = (\hat{\mathbf L}\cdot\hat{\mathbf N})\,\hat{\mathbf N}$$

Let \(\mathbf c\) be the sideways component: \(\mathbf c = \hat{\mathbf L} - \mathbf N'\). On the other side of the normal that component is \(-\mathbf c\). The reflection is the projection plus this flipped component:

$$\hat{\mathbf R} = \mathbf N' + (-\mathbf c) = 2(\hat{\mathbf L}\cdot\hat{\mathbf N})\,\hat{\mathbf N} - \hat{\mathbf L}$$
Step by step

1. Project \(\hat{\mathbf L}\) onto \(\hat{\mathbf N}\) → \(\mathbf N'\).   2. The leftover sideways part is \(\mathbf c = \hat{\mathbf L}-\mathbf N'\).   3. Flip it to \(-\mathbf c\) on the far side.   4. Add: \(\hat{\mathbf R} = \mathbf N' - \mathbf c = 2(\hat{\mathbf L}\cdot\hat{\mathbf N})\hat{\mathbf N} - \hat{\mathbf L}\).

Section 07 · Assembling the model

The Phong light model

We have an intensity \(I\), a diffuse term and a specular term. Because we are working with an additive model of light, we simply add them. The shared intensity \(I\) factors out:

$$C = I\big(\,k_d(\hat{\mathbf N}\cdot\hat{\mathbf L}) + k_s(\hat{\mathbf V}\cdot\hat{\mathbf R})^{\,n}\,\big)$$

This is one of the most popular simple light models in graphics — the Phong light model (also called a reflection model or material model; the terms are interchangeable). It transforms our matte teapot into a believable shiny ceramic surface.

Remember

Phong is a local illumination model: it lights each surface from the light source alone. It knows nothing about how objects in the scene affect one another (a nearby mirror, say, is ignored). Local models run fast on the GPU but have real limits.

Section 08 · A better specular

Blinn–Phong & the halfway vector

Phong has a flaw at extreme viewing angles — grazing angles in graphics-speak. When the view direction becomes nearly perpendicular to the normal (looking at the teapot almost from underneath), the specular term breaks down and the highlight falls apart.

The deeper fix is to change the question. Phong asks: "is the viewer looking down the reflection ray?" Instead we should ask: "is the surface oriented so the viewer sees a reflection?" We answer it with the halfway vector \(\hat{\mathbf H}\), sitting exactly between \(\hat{\mathbf L}\) and \(\hat{\mathbf V}\), and measure the angle \(\omega\) between \(\hat{\mathbf H}\) and the normal \(\hat{\mathbf N}\).

The intuition: when \(\hat{\mathbf V}\) is aligned with the reflection, \(\hat{\mathbf H}\) aligns with \(\hat{\mathbf N}\) → full highlight. And \(\hat{\mathbf H}\) is dead cheap to compute — just add the two vectors and normalise:

$$\hat{\mathbf H} = \frac{\hat{\mathbf L}+\hat{\mathbf V}}{|\hat{\mathbf L}+\hat{\mathbf V}|} \qquad I_s = k_s(\hat{\mathbf H}\cdot\hat{\mathbf N})^{\,n} = k_s\cos^{\,n}\!\omega$$

One vector add plus a normalise — much faster on the GPU than building \(\hat{\mathbf R}\) with dot products and additions. This is the Blinn–Phong light model. It stays stable at grazing angles, and side-by-side it handles reflections slightly better — notice the teapot's handle and the area around the cap.

Local illumination, again

Both Phong and Blinn–Phong are local illumination models — they light an object from the source but ignore inter-object effects. They run first and fast on the GPU. These models are the foundation that newer techniques like ray tracing and other light-transport methods build on.

Section 09 · Finishing the equation

Multiple lights & the ambient return

We assumed one light. With more lights, the additive model lets us simply sum their contributions — as long as we don't push past white in low dynamic range, or the colour data structure breaks down.

And remember ambient light from the start? Local illumination ignores object-to-object bounce, but ambient light fills that role: a uniform term that lifts every point out of pure black. We add it at the end. It has its own intensity \(I_a\) and its own colour \(k_a\) — that colour lets ambient be tinted (a purple room casts a purple wash over everything).

$$C = I\big(\,k_d(\hat{\mathbf N}\cdot\hat{\mathbf L}) + k_s(\hat{\mathbf H}\cdot\hat{\mathbf N})^{\,n}\,\big) + k_a I_a$$

Add that ambient term and the teapot finally reads as proper ceramic. Everything from the chapter maps directly onto a short fragment shader: the code mirrors the equation line for line.

Section 10 · Two loose ends

Light shapes & distance

Lights aren't always points

We assumed point lights. Real sources have shape — area lights, spotlights, distant (directional) lights — and each interacts with surfaces differently. Conceptually the model is unchanged; the maths just shifts slightly to account for the geometry.

Distance attenuation

The other gap: what happens as the light moves away from the object? Everything darkens, because intensity falls with distance. To complete the equation we scale by some function of distance \(d_{att}\). This is distance attenuation — explored further in the workshops. Nothing difficult, but good to know it's there.

$$C = \frac{I}{d_{att}}\big(\,k_d(\hat{\mathbf N}\cdot\hat{\mathbf L}) + k_s(\hat{\mathbf H}\cdot\hat{\mathbf N})^{\,n}\,\big) + k_a I_a$$
The turning point

If there's one place in this course where the pieces of the puzzle come together, it's this. These shading models are the basis of techniques you've heard of — ray tracing and other light-transport methods — and are essential groundwork for any advanced rendering.

2022 · Q22 & Q23 · 4 marks each

Q22. Describe the shortcomings of the simple shading algorithm and how Gouraud shading addresses them.
Q23. Describe the shortcomings of Gouraud shading and how Phong shading addresses them.

▸ Show answer pointers
Simple/flat → Gouraud: flat shading computes one colour per face, giving faceted, blocky surfaces with visible edges. Gouraud computes lighting per vertex and interpolates the colour across the triangle, giving smooth gradients. Gouraud → Phong: because Gouraud interpolates colour, specular highlights falling inside a triangle are missed or smeared (highlights stick to vertices). Phong shading interpolates the normal across the surface and evaluates the lighting equation per fragment, so highlights appear correctly mid-triangle.

$$C = \sum_{\text{lights}}\frac{I}{d_{att}}\Big(k_d(\hat{\mathbf N}\cdot\hat{\mathbf L}) + k_s(\hat{\mathbf H}\cdot\hat{\mathbf N})^{n}\Big) + k_a I_a$$
