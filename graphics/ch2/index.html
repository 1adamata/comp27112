01 How we see

Light, the eye & photoreceptors

To talk about computer-generated images, we first need to know how a human perceives them.

A bulb shoots rays into the scene. When a ray hits an object, the object absorbs some wavelengths based on its colour and material, and reflects the rest toward the eye. That is why the teapot is perceived as pink at a given point — unless the object is a black hole.

Two kinds of photoreceptor

  • Cones — see colour with high accuracy. Three types tuned to Short, Medium, Long wavelengths (≈ RGB).
  • Rods — twilight / dark vision. No colour, but excellent temporal resolution.

The number of cone types depends on the species — dogs differ from us, and the mantis shrimp can have up to 16. Most humans have 3; some people can have 4.

We are not equally sensitive to all colours

A puzzle worth thinking about

Colour-blind people usually struggle with pairs — red/green, or less commonly blue/yellow. But if one cone channel failed, you'd expect a single missing colour, not a pair. And we have no "yellow" cone, yet we detect yellow extremely well. Why?

The answer is that vision is far more than raw RGB. Lots of processing happens after the retina. The colour-opponent channel theory partly explains both colour blindness and our strong yellow perception. (Not examinable — but interesting.)

Eccentricity, the fovea & peripheral vision

  • The fovea is packed with colour cones; the rest of the retina is mostly rods.
  • Periphery has two orders of magnitude fewer receptors — otherwise the visual cortex would be enormous.
  • Detail drops as eccentricity from the fovea increases → we lose spatial detail in peripheral vision.
  • At night, look slightly beside a dim star to use peripheral rods — stare straight at it and it vanishes.
02 Capturing light

Sensors, primaries & additive colour

From wavelengths to a value

Each sensor cell records a light intensity. The combination of wavelengths reaching a region is recorded as a colour. White light through a prism splits into a rainbow — and the reverse, composing wavelengths, gives us colour.

(Yes — that prism is also the Dark Side of the Moon cover.)

RGB additive colour

Tuples in [0,1]

1 means full intensity in a channel:

  • (1,0,0) = red   (0,1,0) = green   (0,0,1) = blue
  • (1,1,1) = white (recomposing all the light)
  • (0,0,0) = black — the surface absorbs light and reflects nothing (the cow's black patches)

A true (0,0,0) would need a perfect absorber — essentially a black hole.

03 Scene → pixels

Rasterisation, resolution & PPI

Pixel = Picture Element

  • Each pixel holds RGB data, each channel in [0,1].
  • We can't store infinite continuous values, so we quantise: 8 bits → 256 levels (0–255) per channel.
  • If hardware can't do colour (no RGB LEDs/plasma), you see greyscale — pixels carry a single value.

Pixels are point samples, not little squares

But our eyes have a resolution too — past a point, a tiny high-PPI image holds detail your retina simply can't recover.

How big is a raw image?

Uncompressed (e.g. PPM) stores every pixel independently. Memory = width × height × channels × bits. A 12-megapixel RGB image needs ~36 MB raw.

04 Storing pixels

Scanlines, planes & Z-order

Where scanlines come from

Rasterisation descends from cathode-ray-tube TVs, where a beam swept across the screen line by line. A mis-calibrated analog signal produced that trippy noise — the little hook structures are the line endings (new scanlines).

Interleaved vs planar

Interleaved keeps each pixel together. Planar separates channels into tables — handy for compression or colour-system swaps.

Want a gloomier movie? With planar storage you tweak just the blue plane instead of touching every pixel tuple.

Swizzling & Z-order curves

Why bother? Spatial coherency

In a scene (and in your room) stuff clusters together — a busy desk, then lots of empty wall. Scanline order scatters a single object across distant memory.

Z-order reads pixels in a localised order, exploiting that clustering to speed up searches or region edits.

05 Dynamic range

From clipping to HDR

Dynamic range = the difference between the brightest and darkest parts of an image.

LDR — Low Dynamic Range

  • Values live in [0,1]; white is the maximum.
  • Typically 8 bits/channel (16 if pushing it; 1 & 24 also exist, 24 rare).
  • Multiplying everything ×2 just clamps the bright pixels to white → lost values.

HDR — High Dynamic Range

  • Can store information brighter than white (go above 1).
  • 16 / 32 bits per channel.
  • Great for matching real life — but images become 2–4× bigger (why film frames render slowly).

06 File formats

Storing & squeezing images

PPM (Portable Pixmap)

  • Header = width, then raw pixel values. Uncompressed.
  • 8 / 16 / 24 bits/channel · LDR · stores each pixel independently.
  • → very large files, even at low resolution. Good for image processing, bad for games.
  • .bmp is similar but a bit more space-efficient and better maintained.

PNG (Portable Network Graphics)

  • Lossless compression — convert PPM→PNG→PPM and recover the original.
  • Traditionally LDR; HDR added in 2025. LDR uses 8/16 bits.
  • Per-image colour tables generated → efficient, supports opacity. Great for logos & pixel art with few colours.
  • Colours not in the table get clamped to the nearest entry → some loss there.

JPEG (Joint Photographic Experts Group)

  • Lossy compression — the original cannot be perfectly recovered.
  • Exploits human perception: we spot edges well, so edges stay crisp while smoother areas are compressed.
  • LDR · uses different colour formats for compression · up to 24-bit colour.
  • Often compress 40×+ before differences are obvious.

Raster vs Vector

Why does the cow stay sharp when you zoom?

Zoom into a raster image and you hit blocky box-filter patterns (aliasing). But a vector graphic (SVG) is defined by mathematical functions telling the GPU how to draw shapes.

Zoom in or out and the computer recomputes the geometry — no blocky pixels, scales infinitely.

07 Perception & gamma

Gamma correction

Human brightness perception is non-linear: we are far more sensitive to changes in dark tones than in bright ones.

The transfer function

We map stored values through a power curve before display:

Vout = A · Vinγ

A common γ = 2.2, chosen from empirical studies. This packs more representable levels into the darker tones the eye cares about — without losing detail. Most LDR displays do this automatically.

Interactive · gamma transfer curve

Bend the curve, remap the tones

The top strip is a linear ramp (0→1). The bottom strip is the same ramp encoded with v1/γ. As γ rises, more steps are spent on the dark end — exactly where your eye is most sensitive.

Does HDR still need gamma correction?

Yes. Even with many bits, we want a consistent experience across displays and drivers, so we apply perceptual quantisation — a valuable tool even in HDR imaging.
