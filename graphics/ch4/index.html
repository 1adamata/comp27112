3D Viewing
& the Camera

How a 3D scene becomes a flat 2D image — coordinate frames, hierarchical modelling, the camera analogy, the duality of moving the world vs. the camera, and the orthographic projection that ends the pipeline.

⌖ Modelling ⇄ Viewing f̂ = normalise(E − C) TC = T₂T₁ … and there is no camera
01 — REFRESHER

Vectors live in a frame

A point p only has coordinates relative to a coordinate frame. Move the frame and the same physical point gets different numbers — yet the two descriptions are linked.

Remember the core idea from the transformations lecture: a transformation is fully described by what it does to the basis vectors. If we transform the basis vectors of one frame, the vector we read off is the very same vector expressed from the other frame.

T(v) = Σ  ci · T(b⃗i)   // transform the basis ⇒ transform the vector
Relativity intuition. Sitting in a rocket, the rocket feels still and the planet seems to rush past. To people on the planet, you are the one moving. Neither view is "wrong" — it is purely a choice of frame. That same duality drives everything in this lecture.
02 — STRUCTURE

Hierarchical modelling

Real models have many parts. Instead of placing every vertex in world coordinates by hand, we give each part its own local frame, referred to from its parent and ultimately from world coordinates.

  • Move the teapot's local frame and every component follows together — topology is preserved automatically.
  • Artists build a part once, in convenient local coordinates, then it is reused and placed via a transform.
  • Without a convention of origins, dropping a model into a different world would break it completely.
03 — HOMOGENEOUS COORDS

Position vs direction vectors

Homogeneous coordinates let us flag a vector as a position or a direction by the bottom component. A direction vector has a 0 there, so translation cancels out — it keeps its orientation no matter where the frame sits.

Why it matters. The teapot's "up" direction (lid pointing up) should stay attached to the model, not the frame. Encode it as a direction vector and a rotating frame won't drag it around. Later, when light bounces off a surface, we want surface normals governed by the surface — not by the orientation of whatever frame we happen to use.
04 — THE CAMERA ANALOGY

Setting the scene, pointing a camera

Computer graphics borrows from photography: arrange objects, point a camera, take the shot. Two distinct transforms get us there.

  • Modelling transform — places each object in world coordinates. No single formula; it's whatever combination of translate / rotate / scale the artist chooses.
  • Viewing (camera) transform — tells the pipeline from where to render. This is common across every API and engine, and is the real subject of this lecture.
05 — THE BIG IDEA

The duality of modelling & viewing

Rotate the object by 60°, or rotate the camera by −60° (the inverse): the resulting photo is essentially identical. You can move the world or move the camera and get the same image.

Which one is the rotated Pringles?

Look at the two lids. Can you actually tell which can was turned and which camera was turned?

That's exactly the lesson: aside from minor lighting cues, you can't reliably tell. Rotating the object and inversely rotating the camera produce the same framing. This equivalence is what lets us implement the viewing transform as a transform applied to the whole scene.
06 — CAMERA COORDINATE SYSTEM

Building the camera's axes

The camera lives at eye point E, looks at target C, and needs three orthonormal axes: f̂ forwardû upŝ side

① Forward

Subtract: E − C points back from the target toward the eye. Normalise it.

f̂ = normalise(E − C)

② Up — but be careful

We supply a desired up vector v̂, but we can't assume it's perpendicular to f̂. The fix is a pair of cross products, so the result is guaranteed orthonormal.

③ Side, then re-derive Up

ŝ = normalise(f̂ × v̂)
û = normalise(f̂ × ŝ)

07 — WHAT THE CAMERA SEES

The viewing volume

Think of the camera as enclosing a volume of space — a cube here. We want to take the scene and flatten it onto the cube face nearest the camera (the future 2D image, i.e. f = 0).

Portal aside. In a game like Portal, the main camera renders to your display, but every portal is essentially a mini-camera showing a different view of the scene. It's convenient to keep the main camera transform at the world origin so the math at the end of the pipeline stays simple.
08 — THE VIEWING TRANSFORM

Moving the camera to the origin

The goal: relocate the camera to the world origin with its axes aligned to the world axes. That's two transforms — a translation, then a rotate/align step.

Because we're in homogeneous coordinates, we just multiply them into one composite matrix. By convention this sets the f̂ axis to point toward −z.

TC = T₂ · T₁   // the viewing (camera) transform

The inverse closes the loop

The inverse TC−1 does two jobs. It can send the camera back to its original pose — or, applied to the scene, it moves the whole world (cow and all) so we get exactly the image we'd have gotten by moving the camera. That's the duality, made concrete.

09 — FLATTENING THE WORLD

There is no camera

The camera is a skeuomorphism — a helpful metaphor. In reality the GPU never builds a camera; it just takes 3D scene data and projects it onto a flat 2D display.

Orthographic projection

Put the display at the origin and simply set every z coordinate to 0. The matrix zeroes the third row. Done — the crudest possible projection.

⌖ Interactive — collapse the z axis

Side view: horizontal = depth (z), vertical = height (y). Press project to send every point onto the plane at z = 0. Notice depth ordering is destroyed.

Why orthographic isn't enough.
  • It flattens the entire space, including things above or below the view.
  • Sometimes the projection shouldn't be a plain rectangle (think Portal).
  • Most importantly, killing z destroys depth — so we lose which surface is in front. A transparent glass in front of Suzanne couldn't tint what's behind it, and we'd have no idea what occludes what.

The next Viewing lecture fixes all of this: perspective projection that preserves z for correct ordering, plus clipping to the viewing volume — and it feeds straight into the GPU pipeline.
