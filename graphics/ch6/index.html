Surfaces & Triangles

In graphics we never model solid objects — we model their thin surfaces. This chapter walks from the humble plane all the way to triangle meshes, subdivision and 3D scanning: how we describe a shape, store it efficiently, and feed it to a GPU.

00 · The big idea

A model is a hollow shell

Move a camera inside almost any 3D model — a teapot, a cow, a scanned sneaker — and you find nothing there. You see the textures flip around, but the interior is empty. That is because graphics represents the surface of an object, not its volume. Everything in this chapter is a different way of answering one question: how do we describe a surface?

“In graphics we are modelling surfaces — and that will be the topic of this video.”

01 · The simplest surface

Planes

Take a flat wooden floor. Give it a coordinate system with the origin in its middle. Any vector that lies in an axis-aligned plane has its height component zero — so the plane is simply every point where z = 0.

v = xyz ,   f(v) = z = 0

The general plane is more interesting. Writing the dot of a coefficient vector with the position vector, and adding an offset, gives the implicit equation of a plane:

f(v) = ax + by + cz + d = n̂ · v + d = 0 implicit plane — n̂ is the unit normal, d is an offset

That little vector [a b c] is the normal n̂: a unit vector pointing in the plane's "up" direction, describing its orientation. When the plane is horizontal the normal happens to align with z — but tilt the plane and z no longer points along the normal. Because the dot product of two perpendicular vectors is zero, every vector lying in the plane is automatically perpendicular to n̂. The scalar d simply slides the plane back and forth along the normal; d = 0 when the plane passes through the origin.

02 · Two ways to write a shape

Implicit vs explicit

The sphere is the classic example. Its implicit form is the familiar equation set to zero — which, since v·v = x²+y²+z², compresses neatly:

f(v) = x2+y2+z2−r2 = v·v − r2 = 0 implicit sphere

The explicit (parametric) form instead tells you exactly where each point lands, sweeping two angles like longitude and latitude:

x = x0 + r sin θ cos φ    y = y0 + r sin θ cos φ    z = z0 + r cos θ explicit / parametric sphere
Why keep the implicit form? Explicit is easy to render — you can directly compute every vertex location. Implicit is harder to fill in (you only know the surface equation) but has a magical property: an instant inside / outside test. For any point's vector v, the sign of f(v) says it all — and this is the basis of algorithms such as ray tracing.

Even something as intricate as the Stanford bunny can be written as one implicit function — but change anything about it and you'd need a team of mathematicians. Using implicit or explicit functions to build shapes is called procedural modelling.

03 · Procedural modelling

Fractals & self-similarity

Procedural modelling shines for natural things. A romanesco is essentially a fractal: zoom into one floret and it looks like the whole vegetable — the shape repeats recursively at every scale. The same trick of controlled randomness builds entire mountain ranges and has done since the earliest days of CG (the Star Trek II "Genesis" demo used fractal terrain).

04 · Rule-based growth

L-systems

L-systems (Lindenmayer systems) are a procedural method where you define recursive rewrite rules that are applied every iteration — so they are a form of fractal too. They are wonderful for foliage, but also for snowflakes and other self-similar models.

05 · Smooth controllable surfaces

NURBS & Bézier patches

For smooth, designer-controlled surfaces we use Bézier patches (also called tensor-product spline patches) and NURBS — Non-Uniform Rational B-Splines. The points floating outside the surface are control points: weights that pull the surface around. Drag a control point and the surface follows. This gives excellent local control and is popular in CAD and automotive modelling. Fun fact: the Utah teapot was originally built from Bézier patches.

Watch the acronym NURBS stands for Non-Uniform Rational B-Splines. (The lecture audio slips and says “non-directional uniform” — the slide and the literature use Non-Uniform Rational.)
06 · What games actually want

Polygons

Bézier maths is lovely, but for games we want simple polygonal shapes: easy to render and easy to control locally. So a bunny becomes a polygonal soup — a loose pile of polygons. A polygon is precisely:

  • an ordered set of vertices (v1 … vn),
  • a set of edges between each consecutive pair (e1 … en),
  • and a point P is inside the polygon if it is bounded by those edges.

This already suits GPUs, which are built around vertices.

07 · Orientation is everything

Polygon normals

Normals are the key to rendering correctly. To find the normal of an arbitrary polygon, define two edge vectors, take their cross product (which is perpendicular to both), then normalise so it becomes a unit vector:

N = E1 × −E2     n̂ = normalise(E1 × −E2) cross product → perpendicular → unit normal

Why bother? Back-face culling: GPUs throw away polygons whose normal faces away from the camera, for efficiency. Get the normal direction wrong and you see artefacts — the front face gets culled instead of the back.

The catch Computing a normal is only easy for a planar shape. A quad's four vertices need not be coplanar, and polygons can be concave (an interior angle > 180°). GPUs love convex shapes — “APIs don't like these!”

08 · The humble hero

Why everything becomes a triangle

The solution to non-planar quads and awkward concave polygons is the triangle. Three points always define a single plane, so a triangle is planar by definition — which makes its normal (and almost everything else) trivial to compute. Look at the wireframe of any rendered scene and you'll see it is mostly triangles, from a Kilburn office render to Manchester University's reconstruction of a garage crime-scene for Greater Manchester Police.

We can model with quads, curves and splines — but everything is converted into triangles at the end of the pipeline, because GPUs are hyper-optimised for them.

09 · Splitting into triangles

Tessellation

Tessellation takes complex shapes — convex or concave — and splits them into triangles. There is a dedicated tessellation shader in the programmable pipeline, and everything eventually gets tessellated, including that Bézier-patch teapot.

Exam definition Tessellation = splitting a polygon into a set of separate convex polygons (triangles). It is necessary because GPUs render triangle primitives, and because concave/non-planar polygons cannot be rasterised reliably.
10 · Why raw polygons are wasteful

Problems with polygon soup

A naïve list of triangles has three serious problems:

  • Huge waste of space — storing three vertices per triangle independently means 3N vertices for N triangles.
  • Repeated vertices — any vertex is shared by 3–4 polygons, so it is stored again and again.
  • Complete loss of semantics — you know there's a vertex at (x, y, z), but is it part of a cat, a sneaker, or nothing in particular? The structure is gone.
11 · Reusing the shared edge

Triangle strips

Add a triangle that shares an edge with the previous one, and the two vertices of that edge are already in memory. Shuffle the array so vertices flow sequentially and you can tell the API: “render this as a triangle strip.” Now you only need N + 2 vertices for N triangles — almost three times fewer than polygon soup.

N + 2 verticesfor a strip of N triangles

The price is winding order: you read the vertices clockwise (0,1,2), then counter-clockwise (1,2,3), then clockwise again, alternating. As long as the rule for storing and reading is well defined this is fine — but you must be careful. Strips suit landscapes and long meshes; consecutive strips have to be "glued", repeating a few vertices at the joins.

12 · Fanning from a root

Triangle fans

A triangle fan keeps one root vertex and fans triangles out from it. It also costs only N + 2 vertices. Fans suit cone-like or circular meshes, while strips suit landscapes and long meshes — both are about dividing a model into parts so the GPU can render it efficiently.

13 · Structure restored

The polygonal mesh

Raw polygons are poor, but a mesh — a linked group of polygons and smaller meshes with well-defined relationships — is excellent. A mesh is easy to tessellate, lets us cut spatial complexity and vertex repetition, preserves topology, and most importantly gives us back semantics: a strip is recognisably part of the model, not random points in space.

14 · Fast access

Indexed polygons

Modern GPUs aren't as bottlenecked by memory layout, but we still want fast access. The trick: keep one array of vertices, then define elements with their indices — a separate, cheap array of integers that point at the vertices. Relationships become easy to express and very fast to traverse.

OpenGL exposes this through gl.drawElements(gl.TRIANGLES, …) — swap the keyword for TRIANGLE_STRIP or TRIANGLE_FAN as needed. three.js does the same by adding an index attribute to the geometry that tells the shaders how to reach the vertices. Either way you first need a data structure holding the relationships between elements.

15 · Acquiring geometry · I

Subdivision surfaces

One way to build models is to hand-map polygons to real things. Henri Gouraud famously modelled his wife — Mme Gouraud, 1978 — early work that revolutionised graphics. But hand-mapping is hugely resource-intensive and still looks blocky if the polygons are too big.

Subdivision turns a rough shape (say an icosahedron) into a smooth one (a sphere) almost for free. The recipe: sample points along the edges, then average each vertex with its neighbours. Corner vertices drift slightly inward; edge midpoints stay put. Connect the new vertices and a square becomes a rounded square; repeat and it converges to a circle.

In 3D we generally use Catmull–Clark subdivision. Remarkably, even quite different starting polygons (like Blender's Suzanne) converge to similar smooth results. The catch: the polygon count grows rapidly with each level, so you must budget your iterations.

16 · Acquiring geometry · II

Scanning, NeRFs & Gaussian splatting

The other way to get realistic geometry is to capture it.

3D laser scanning

Stanford used a huge laser scanner on Michelangelo's David in Florence. The output is a 3D point cloud; you then reconstruct geometry from it by figuring out how to map triangles onto the points.

Neural Radiance Fields (NeRFs)

Google's NeRFs use volumetric methods to reconstruct geometry from photos taken at multiple angles — sometimes even from a single still image. (You'll meet these properly in third-year advanced graphics.)

Gaussian splatting

The newest approach, Gaussian splatting, is far more efficient to train than a NeRF and is optimised for rasterisation — so we don't build volumetric meshes at all. The trained model drops into the scene effortlessly.

For the exam Know the names and roughly what each does — laser scan → point cloud → reconstruction; NeRF → volumetric reconstruction from photos; Gaussian splatting → efficient, rasterisation-friendly capture. You don't need the internal maths of NeRFs or splats.

Exam questions & how to answer

Real questions from past COMP27112 papers that map onto this chapter. Expand each for a model-answer skeleton — illustrate every answer with clear diagrams, as the rubric demands.

2017 Q8Given a triangle ABC, explain how to compute the triangle's normal vector. (2 marks)+
Model answer
  • Form two edge vectors from a shared vertex, e.g. E₁ = B − A and E₂ = C − A.
  • Take the cross product N = E₁ × E₂ — the result is perpendicular to both edges, i.e. to the triangle's plane.
  • Normalise: n̂ = N / |N| to get a unit normal.
  • The winding order (CW/CCW) of A,B,C decides which way n̂ points — fix a consistent convention.
2017 Q7What is polygon soup? Discuss whether you think it is a good or bad idea. (2 marks)+
Model answer
  • Polygon soup = an unstructured collection of polygons with no defined relationships between them.
  • Bad: huge memory waste, vertices duplicated across adjacent polygons, and total loss of semantics/topology (you can't tell what object a vertex belongs to).
  • Arguably fine for: simple throw-away geometry or when a mesh structure isn't needed — but for real models a structured mesh (strips, fans, indexed) is far better.
2016 Q3bWith illustrated examples, explain: (i) polygon normal vector, (ii) polygon soup, (iv) mesh data structure. (2 + 2 + 4 marks)+
Model answer
  • (i) Polygon normal vector: unit vector perpendicular to the polygon's plane; from edge vectors via cross product + normalise; defines orientation and drives lighting & back-face culling.
  • (ii) Polygon soup: unstructured polygons — wasteful, duplicated vertices, no semantics (see above).
  • (iv) Mesh data structure: a linked group of polygons sharing vertices via an index/element array over a single vertex array; preserves topology + semantics; supports strips/fans; reduces storage. Draw a small worked example: vertex list + index list → triangles.
2018 Q4.5Describe what is meant by tessellation, and when it is necessary. (2 marks)+
Model answer
  • Tessellation = splitting a complex polygon (convex or concave) into a set of simple convex polygons — in practice, triangles.
  • Necessary because GPUs render triangle primitives, and because non-planar / concave polygons can't be rasterised or normal-computed reliably. Everything is tessellated before final rendering.
2019 Q2.21Polygon ABCD contains a hole EFG. What process enables ABCD to be correctly rendered by OpenGL, and what is its effect?+
Model answer
  • Apply tessellation: OpenGL cannot render a polygon with a hole (or a concave/complex outline) directly.
  • The tessellator decomposes ABCD-with-hole into a set of convex triangles whose union is the original filled region minus the hole.
  • Effect: ABCD becomes a triangle mesh that rasterises correctly, with the hole EFG left unfilled.
2016 Q3dYou must build a walk-through 3D model of Kilburn LT 1.1 — how would you collect the data? (2 marks)+
Model answer
  • 3D scanning: a laser scanner captures the room as a point cloud; reconstruct surfaces by fitting triangles to the points.
  • Alternatively photo-based capture (multiple angles) reconstructed with NeRF / photogrammetry / Gaussian splatting.
  • Combine with manual modelling for furniture; tessellate & build an indexed mesh for the walk-through engine.
