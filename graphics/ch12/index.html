01 — THE BIG IDEA

Graphics is an approximation

We have come a very long way toward photorealistic rendering. But everything in the graphics world is only an approximation of the real world — keep that in mind for this whole chapter.

We can't build GPUs with infinite resources that simulate every light particle bouncing off every material. So instead we invent hacks that squeeze the best possible image out of the hardware we actually have. The catch: every time we improve one thing, we usually break another technique that was working fine. Rendering is a constant game of catch-up.

Exam favourite

"Graphics images are always approximations"

This exact framing appears across multiple past papers (2016, 2018, 2019). It is the thesis of the entire lecture, so it is worth being able to argue it cold with concrete examples (aliasing, finite polygon counts, discrete pixels, limited bandwidth, LOD swaps).

02 — SAMPLING & ALIASING

Why a smooth wave goes wrong

Before pixels, start with sound — it is the same maths, and easier to hear.

A sound wave is, in theory, perfectly continuous. But a microphone can only capture it as discrete samples taken at some sampling rate. We can approximate an infinitely smooth wave, but we can never represent it perfectly — exactly the same reason we don't get truly photorealistic rendering.

For a 5 Hz signal sampled at ~32 samples/second, the reconstruction is excellent. Lower the rate and it degrades gracefully — until you cross a threshold where the samples no longer describe the original wave at all. The reconstructed signal becomes a completely different, lower-frequency wave. That phantom is aliasing.

Left: too few samples to capture the wave. Right (red): the alias — a frequency that was never there. Aliasing literally hallucinates frequencies.

03 — THE RULE

Nyquist–Shannon

There is a precise threshold for "enough samples", and it has a name.

Sampling theorem

fs > 2·fmax

To faithfully sample a signal, the sampling rate must be more than twice the highest frequency present. From this we derive the Nyquist frequency = fs/2: the maximum frequency that can be represented faithfully with a given sampling rate.

Nyquist–Shannon. Sample at more than twice the max frequency; the Nyquist frequency is half the sampling rate.

Humans hear roughly 20 Hz–20 kHz, so recording devices sample at around 40+ kHz (twice the upper bound). A dolphin, hearing past 150 kHz, would need sampling above ~300 kHz for a good listening experience.

The wagon-wheel effect

Sample exactly at the Nyquist frequency and the reconstruction can flat-line: samples keep landing on the same phase of the wave. This is why a helicopter's rotor — or a car wheel on film — can appear frozen or even spin backwards when the camera's frame rate lines up with the blade frequency.

04 — BACK TO PIXELS

Pixels are samples, not squares

The single most important idea from week 1, finally cashing in.

A pixel is not a little square. It is a dimensionless point sample, represented using a box filter. The word "sample" was stressed all course for exactly this moment: if we point-sample something continuous, we get aliasing — the same demon as the sound wave.

In images, the Nyquist frequency is set by the spacing between pixels (true of any array of receptors). At a hard edge, the signal jumps almost vertically — a step function in the spatial domain. To reproduce a perfectly vertical edge with waves you'd need to sample at infinity, which is impossible. So instead we smooth the jump.

Smoothing the spatial step is exactly what texture filtering already did for us — closer to reality and fewer artefacts.

The problem

Jagged edges = under-sampling

Solid textured areas look fine, but around edges the box-filter sampling produces blocky, jagged "stair-steps". Picture a pixel grid laid over the image: the rails of this roller-coaster fall apart at every diagonal.

05 — THE SOLUTIONS

Anti-aliasing

Aliasing is an under-sampling problem, so the fixes all come down to: get more information, cleverly.

SSAA — Super-Sample Anti-Aliasing

The brute-force fix: just sample at sub-pixel level. Run rasterisation and the fragment shader for every sub-sample, then average. Edges get blended, smooth colours.

Cost

Brutally expensive

Both rasterisation and the fragment shader run at every sub-sample, so cost scales linearly with the sample count: N× (Rasterisation + Shading). At 4K with 4× or 16× sampling, that's an enormous amount of redundant work. Old games expose it as a setting; modern engines mostly avoid it.

MSAA — Multi-Sample Anti-Aliasing

The key insight: you don't need to shade every sub-sample — only test coverage & depth there. So MSAA does the z-buffer test at multiple sub-positions to compute a fractional coverage, but the fragment shader runs once per covered pixel. Much cheaper, and it still smooths geometry edges beautifully — especially with overlapping triangles.

SSAA
Rasterise + shade every sub-sample. Highest quality, highest cost.
MSAA
Sub-sample coverage & depth only; shade once per pixel. Great for edges, far cheaper.

FXAA & TAA — the post-process era

FXAA (Fast Approximate AA) is a cheap post-processing blur that detects and softens jagged edges. Low cost, compatible with almost any renderer, but can look soft/blurry.

TAA (Temporal AA) is one of the most popular methods today. Each frame applies a tiny sub-pixel jitter to the projection matrix, then accumulates samples across frames — a high effective sample rate for very little per-frame cost. The downside is ghosting and lost detail in motion (look at the specular highlights smearing around the eyes).

DSR & DLSS — the modern frontier

NVIDIA's DSR (Dynamic Super Resolution) is essentially SSAA but driver-level, scene-aware and hyper-optimised. DLSS (Deep Learning Super Sampling) uses machine learning — promising, but it can hallucinate detail, occasionally inventing crispness that produces its own kind of aliasing.

06 — SHADE LESS, SMARTLY

Variable Rate Shading

We've stopped overloading rasterisation. Can we optimise the shading itself?

Shading is one of the most expensive operations in graphics. Do we really need to run the fragment shader once for every pixel? Variable Rate Shading (VRS) — NVIDIA's term; AMD has equivalents — says no. In perceptually important regions we shade per-pixel; elsewhere we shade in coarse tiles, e.g. one shader call per 4×4 = 16 pixels.

Analogy

A painter's brushes

Fine detail → fine brush, careful strokes. Background or sky → broad strokes that cover lots of canvas fast. VRS hands the GPU both brushes and lets it choose per region.

How do we know where to be coarse?

This is where graphics meets biology. Our retinas have a finite number of photoreceptors, spaced non-uniformly — and that spacing sets the maximum sampling rate of the eye. From it we can build a mathematical model of vision: the contrast sensitivity function (CSF). Where frequency is too high or contrast too low, we simply can't resolve detail — our eyes are doing texture filtering for us.

Feed these perceptual models in as oracles and you get saliency maps: predictions of where users will notice detail. Allocating render resources by saliency is called perceptually-driven rendering. In VR with eye-tracking, the gaze point gets fine shading (foveated rendering) while the periphery goes coarse — you can also drop MIP levels there to cut texture work.

07 — GO DEEPER: GEOMETRY

Back-face culling

Now we move up the pipeline into the vertex shader. The deeper you optimise, the bigger the savings.

In the vertex shader we have the vertex normals, and we know the camera position (vector v — we need it for shading anyway). Take a triangle's normal n̂ and dot it with the view vector:

v · n̂ > 0
Vectors point roughly the same way → triangle faces the camera → keep it.
v · n̂ = 0
Perpendicular — edge-on.
v · n̂ < 0
Normal points away → on a solid (opaque) object this face is invisible → discard it.

For a closed opaque mesh that discards roughly 50% of triangles — half as much barycentric interpolation and shading — and the price is a single dot product. Enormous saving for almost nothing.

Watch out

Winding order matters

Culling relies on consistent winding order (your right/left-handed convention). Get it wrong and you cull all the visible faces instead, leaving you staring at the inside of the model. Flip the winding in the demo above to see the artefact.

08 — BEFORE THE GPU EVEN STARTS

Frustum culling & Level of Detail

"We need to go deeper" — back to the CPU, where data is read from memory and pushed to the GPU.

The CPU→GPU hand-off is a massive bottleneck. Often it isn't computation at all — it's literally bandwidth: not enough capacity on the physical connections to feed the pipeline fast enough. So can we send less data?

Frustum culling

We already define the camera's viewing frustum (near plane, far plane, field of view) before the vertex shader runs. So we can run a trivial, cheap test on the CPU: is this object even inside the frustum? If not, don't send it into the pipeline at all. Objects that are never visible never cost a single pipeline stage — which is exactly why it's so effective.

Why two planes?

Near plane & far plane

The near plane stops geometry sitting inside the camera from projecting incorrectly; the far plane bounds the depth range so z-buffer precision isn't wasted on things too distant to matter.

Trade-off

Popping artefacts

Cull too aggressively and objects visibly snap into existence as you turn — jarring "popping". The fix: pre-load data in a slightly larger area than the strict frustum so things are ready before they're needed.

Level of Detail (LOD)

A statue right at the near plane deserves full polygons. The same statue far across the scene? Render a lower-poly version — the silhouette survives and you save huge amounts of computation. Most engine assets ship with several pre-built LOD meshes; the basic algorithm just checks distance from camera and picks a level.

Continuous LOD & Nanite

What if we don't pre-bake the levels? The tessellation shader — the part of the pipeline that divides objects into triangles — can generate meshes on the fly. Adaptively generated detail is continuous LOD.

Unreal Engine 5's Nanite takes this furthest: the LOD decision moves from CPU to GPU, becomes per-cluster, continuous, and driven by screen-space error rather than raw camera distance. This is virtualised geometry — and research here is far from finished.

Tessellation — exam definition

Splitting a polygon into a set of separate convex polygons

Needed because hardware works best with simple convex primitives (triangles); concave polygons and polygons-with-holes must be broken down before rendering.
