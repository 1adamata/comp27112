Why this chapter

The main villain is linear algebra

We all know what modern graphics can do. To understand what's really going on, we go deeper than engines or GPUs and think abstractly. There's some trig and geometry here — but the recurring antagonist is linear algebra.

If your gut reaction is to run, that's fair. The pay-off is huge though: these concepts come back again and again across both the graphics and the computer-vision halves of the course. Let them sink in once and the rest gets easier.

Today's cast: points, vectors, matrices and coordinates. The first two are geometric entities; coordinates are just numbers describing positions; matrices are the interesting ones we'll keep coming back to.

Bold, lowercase, and stacked

Flip a vector on its side with a little T and you've transposed it — every row becomes a column (and vice-versa):

Points vs Vectors · the big one

A point is where. A vector is how far.

Points are absolute locations. Vectors are displacements. Move Suzanne across the room to eat the cheese: the displacement v between her nose and eyebrow stays identical, but the absolute point P becomes a brand-new P′.

P = (1.0, 1.0)
P′ = (1.0, 1.0)
v = (2.0, 1.0) (never changes)
↳ drag Suzanne to translate the point — watch v hold its shape

A vector doesn't describe an absolute spot — only an offset. So the same v can sit anywhere. Translate the model and you must define a new point:

P   = (px, py) P′ = (px+Δ, py+Δ) v   = unchanged
This distinction saves you a lot of headaches later in the course. Memorise it now.
Frame of reference

A vector means nothing without axes

What does [2 1 0] even mean? 2 what — centimetres? potatoes? A vector only has meaning inside a frame of reference: some axes, in some space, with an origin.

Look closely and the axes themselves have arrows — they are vectors describing the space that vectors live in. We pick axes that are orthonormal:

orthonormal = orthogonal (mutually perpendicular) + normal (each one unit long). Not required in theory — axes can even overlap — but orthonormal axes make trigonometry and calculation easy.

With units fixed, [2 1 0]ᵀ finally means something concrete: travel 2 along x, 1 along y, 0 along z. That tip is where the vector points from the origin.

v = |v| · direction  ·  a geometric quantity: a displacement, not an absolute position

Vector space · operation 1

Scalar multiplication

A vector space comes equipped with two operations. The first: multiply by a scalar. Every coordinate scales by the same number — the tail stays at the origin, only the length changes.

c = 2.0
cv = (4.0, 2.0)
↳ slide c — negative flips the vector through the origin

Multiply v = (2, 1) by 2 and you get the longer 2v = (4, 2). Scalar multiplication is purely about how long the vector is (and which way it points if the scalar is negative).

c·v = (c·xᵥ,  c·yᵥ)
Vector space · operation 2

Addition & the resultant

Add two vectors by adding matching components. Geometrically: lay them tip-to-tail, and the diagonal of the parallelogram is the resultant.

v w v+w
v+w = (3.0, 3.0)
↳ drag the v and w arrow-heads
v + w = xᵥ + x𝓌yᵥ + y𝓌 v − w = xᵥ − x𝓌yᵥ − y𝓌

For subtraction, flip one vector around the origin and add — connect the tail of a to the tip of −b. Same tip-to-tail idea, just with a flipped arrow.

If two vectors are placed tip-to-tail, the resultant runs from the very first tail to the very last tip.
Basis · zero · minus

Every vector is a weighted sum of the axes

The axes x and y are basis vectors. Scale them by numbers (scalars, usually cᵢ) and add — that linear combination can reach any vector in the space.

Linear combination & span

v = Σ cᵢ bᵢ  =  c₁·x + c₂·y

c = scalars · b = basis vectors. With standard (orthonormal) basis vectors, every vector is a linear combination of the axes. The set of all reachable vectors is the span.

Zero vector

No displacement, no force. v + 0 = v and v · 0 = 0

Minus vector

Same vector, flipped through the origin — every coordinate × (−1). −v = (−1)·v

Length

Magnitude & unit vectors

Want the scalar length of a vector? It's just Pythagoras. If that length equals 1, it's a unit vector — incredibly important later.

|v| = √(x² + y²)   2D
|v| = √(x² + y² + z²)   3D

If |v| = 1 we write it v̂ and call it a unit vector. Normalising = scaling a vector to length 1 without changing its direction (divide by its magnitude).

|v| = 5.00
v̂ = (0.60, 0.80, 0.00)
↳ type components — try (3,4,0) for a clean 5
Multiplying vectors · I

The dot product → a number

Multiply matching components and sum. The result is a scalar, not a vector — and it secretly encodes the angle between the two vectors.

θ = 45°
a·b = 0.71
↳ both are unit vectors here, so a·b is exactly cos θ — slide to 90° and it hits 0
a·b = aₓbₓ + a_yb_y + a_zb_z
a·b = |a||b| cos θ

Two unit vectors? Then â·b̂ = cos θ directly — gold, since we deal with unit vectors constantly. Perpendicular vectors give cos 90° = 0, so their dot product is always zero.

If b̂ is a unit vector, a·b̂ is the projection (length d) of a onto b. If b isn't unit, normalise it first by dividing by |b|.

The projection picture comes straight from the trigonometric circle: drop a perpendicular from the tip of a onto b, and the shadow it casts is exactly d = a·b̂.

(The full proof is lovely but this isn't the maths unit — derive it in your own time.)

Multiplying vectors · II

The cross product → a new vector

Where the dot product spits out a scalar, the cross product of two vectors gives a third vector that's perpendicular to both of them.

u × v = w    w ⊥ u,   w ⊥ v

The result w is only a unit vector if u and v both are. Crucially, the direction depends on your handedness convention: swap left-hand ↔ right-hand and w flips to −w.

To flip the result, cross with the negated vector: u × (−v) = −w — the perpendicular now points the opposite way.

Matrices

Grids that do things

Matrices are wildly useful: they can represent vectors, transformations, or solve linear systems. In graphics we mostly meet 2D, 3D and 4D square matrices (they can be any size — ML folks know).

Addition: same size, or no deal

Add matrices by summing entries in matching positions — just like adding vectors component-wise. But the two matrices must be the same dimensions, or the universe refuses to balance.

A + B  →  (aᵢⱼ + bᵢⱼ)

Multiplication: it's almost a function

You can multiply matrices of different sizes — as long as the columns of the first equal the rows of the second. That lets a matrix act on a vector: feed v in, get a brand-new vector out.

A = (this one rotates 90°)
vAv
Av = (-1.0, 2.0)
↳ edit A, drag v's head — the matrix transforms it like a function
A·v = new vector

Think of the matrix as a function that took an input vector, did some operations, and spat out a new one. That's exactly how we'll move, rotate and scale geometry for the rest of the unit.

Quintessential rule — matrix multiplication is NOT commutative. A·v ≠ v·A, and for two matrices A·B ≠ B·A. Order is everything. Remember this for the whole course.

Exam practice

Past-paper questions that lean on this chapter

These COMP27112 exam prompts all stand on the linear-algebra you just covered — vectors, dot & cross products, normals and matrices. Expand each for how to approach it.

2018 · Q4(a)
Give an example of an application of the vector cross product in computer graphics.3 marks (a–c)+
The cross product produces a vector perpendicular to two others — so its classic use is computing a surface / polygon normal. Take two edge vectors of a triangle and cross them; the result is the normal used for lighting and back-face culling.
Key: u × v = w with w ⊥ u and w ⊥ v ⇒ perfect for getting the normal of a flat surface from two of its edges.
2017 · short answer
Given a triangle ABC, explain how to compute the triangle's normal vector.2 marks+
Form two edge vectors from the vertices, e.g. AB = B − A and AC = C − A (point subtraction → displacement vectors). Their cross product AB × AC is perpendicular to the triangle's plane. Normalise it (divide by its magnitude) to get a unit normal.
Pipeline: subtract points → two vectors → cross product → normalise. Handedness of your coordinate system decides which way the normal faces.
2016 · b(i)
Explain, with an illustrated example, what is meant by a polygon normal vector.2 marks+
A polygon normal is a vector perpendicular to the plane of the polygon, indicating which way its surface faces. It's typically a unit vector and is computed via the cross product of two edge vectors. Used for shading, orientation and visibility tests.
Tie-in: "perpendicular" = cross product; "unit" = magnitude 1 after normalising. Both straight from this chapter.
2024 · lighting Q
Two vector expressions appear (forms of N·L and R·V). Define the vectors involved and explain what the expressions achieve.~ short answer+
These are dot products between unit vectors. With N = surface normal, L = direction to light, V = direction to viewer, R = reflected light direction: N·L gives cos of the angle between normal and light (diffuse term), and R·V gives cos of the angle between reflection and viewer (specular term).
Key: for unit vectors, a·b = cos θ. Bigger cosine ⇒ more aligned ⇒ brighter. Perpendicular ⇒ dot = 0 ⇒ no contribution.
2017 · 2022 · short answer
For 3D transformations, why do we use 4×4 matrices instead of 3×3?1 mark+
A 3×3 matrix can encode scales and rotations, but not translation (translation isn't a linear map of the 3 coordinates). Adding a 4th homogeneous coordinate lets a 4×4 matrix encode translations as well as scales and rotations in a single, composable matrix.
Key: uniform 4×4 form ⇒ chain transforms by multiplication. And since matrix multiplication is non-commutative, order matters: applying M1 then M2 then M3 to point P is P′ = M3·M2·M1·P.
quiz · derived
What is the effect of normalising a vector, and what does the dot product of two normalised vectors give?concept+
Normalising scales a vector to magnitude 1 without changing its direction — it now represents pure direction. The dot product of two normalised 3D vectors gives the cosine of the angle between them.
Key: v̂ = v / |v|;   v̂₁ · v̂₂ = cos θ.
