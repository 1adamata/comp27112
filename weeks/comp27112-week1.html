What is image processing?

Two layers here: a textbook one-liner (good for a 1-mark definition), and the deeper IP-vs-Computer-Vision distinction from Tim's Chapter 1 (good for a 3–4 mark question that separates students who skimmed from those who read).

The definitions 1-MARK

Collins: "the manipulation or modification of a digitized image, esp. in order to enhance its quality."

Encyclopaedia Britannica (fuller): a set of computational techniques for analyzing, enhancing, compressing, and reconstructing images. Three components:

  • Importing — capture via scanning or digital photography
  • Analysis & manipulation — using specialised software
  • Output — to a printer or monitor

Applications: astronomy, medicine, industrial robotics, remote sensing by satellites.

Image Processing vs Computer Vision TIM Ch.1

This is the distinction the handout opens with — and it's the cleanest way to define the field.

Image ProcessingComputer Vision
image in → image outimage in → numbers/symbols out
"make a second image that differs from the first""extract numerical or symbolic information"

Tim's worked example — weather radar:
• Convert rainfall-intensity data into a colour image → that's image processing.
• Interpret it to recognise high-rainfall / flood-risk areas or weather systems → that's computer vision.

SOURCE NUANCE

These are young disciplines with no firm agreed boundary — that's why the two overlap. Tim explicitly says alternative definitions exist. Stating "the boundary is least distinct between IP and CV" shows you read past the slide.

🧠 Memory hook — "Pixels in, pixels out vs Pixels in, knowledge out"

IP transforms an image into another image. CV squeezes an image into information (a number, a label, "this is an A"). If the answer is still a picture, it's processing; if the answer is a fact, it's vision.

A short history

Two dates, two drivers. Examiners rarely ask history for many marks, but a date and "why" sentence is cheap insurance and good essay seasoning.

1920s — across the Atlantic TIM

The first digital image appeared in the 1920s: photographs were converted to a numerical format and transmitted by Morse code across the Atlantic by telegraph — the Bartlane system.

  • Photo split into small areas; each given a numerical code based on its brightness distribution.
  • Code transmitted, then photo reconstructed using specially designed printing blocks.
  • Why it mattered: hours to code/transmit/print — but the alternative (steamship) took over a week.
  • Conceptually unchanged for ~50 years; only the hardware got faster.

1960s — the space race

Major advances began in the 1960s. The US and Russian space programmes generated huge amounts of image data that needed:

  • Artefacts removed (clean up sensor/transmission damage)
  • Enhancement for human viewing

Simultaneously, computers finally became powerful enough to store, process and display images in realistic time. Since then, falling hardware cost pushed IP/CV out of government & university labs into everyday life — entertainment, news, medical devices, science.

4

Sample applications

Tim picks three everyday vision systems. Each one secretly teaches a core technique you'll meet later — so they double as a preview. The slides add medical imaging & forensics.

① OCR TIM

Recognising characters in an image. Three rungs of difficulty:

  • Printed text — consistent & regular → recognise by comparison with a prototype/template. (This is template matching — you'll meet it again!) Downside: you need a prototype for every character × case × font × size → a huge number.
  • Hand-printed text — lower quality, irregular → can't template it. Instead use structural features (e.g. "A" = a horizontal stroke + two diagonals). Also hard to segment touching/broken characters.
  • Cursive (handwritten) — not yet solved: too irregular to template, strokes can't be cleanly isolated.

② Biometry TIM

Identifying people from physical characteristics in images: fingerprints, face, and notably the iris.

Iris pipeline:

  • Find the eye in a head-and-shoulders image.
  • Find the iris; resample the annular (ring) region into a rectangle.
  • Resampling uses a constant number of radial samples so pupil dilation (lighting) doesn't change the representation — i.e. size-independent.
  • Numerically transform → a small set of coefficients shown to be unique per person.

③ Aerial photography TIM

Mapping, land-use analysis & change, etc. Captured from hundreds of metres to hundreds of km.

  • Must record camera location & attitude (where + which way it points).
  • Non-vertical camera → oblique, distorted view.
  • Short focal-length (wide) lens distorts periphery — edge objects look larger than they are.
  • Mosaic: either fly a level, straight path at regular intervals, or record positions and resample to fake that path; correct optical distortion too.

Plus, from the unit intro — where IP shows up

Medical imaging (MRI/brain scans) · Forensics (lifting fingerprints off documents) · Geometrical transforms · Camera calibration (fish-eye → corrected) · Noise reduction · Edge detection · Connected-component analysis (counting tadpoles!) · Camera exposure (shutter time vs aperture) · Image compression (JPEG quality vs file size) · Hough transform (finding lines & circles). These are the headlines of the whole IP syllabus.

🧠 Memory hook — "O-B-A" (Oh, Be Aware)

OCR → templates & features · Biometry → resampling to size-independence · Aerial → geometric distortion & mosaicking. Three apps, three foreshadowed techniques.

Image representation — resolution & colour depth

An image is a grid of pixels. Two numbers describe it: how many pixels (resolution) and how many bits each pixel carries (colour depth). Everything about memory size falls out of these.

Resolution

The pixel dimensions width × height. The slide's worked example:

12 megapixels → 4272 × 2848 = 12,166,656 px

"12-megapixel" just means width×height ≈ 12 million. Zooming in far enough reveals the individual pixels — the image is discrete, not continuous. That discreteness is the seed of sampling/aliasing (Week 2+).

Colour depth (bit depth)

How many bits per pixel. 24-bit colour means:

224 = 16,777,216 colours

24-bit = 8 bits each for R, G, B. The number of representable colours is always 2(bit depth). 8-bit greyscale → 2⁸ = 256 grey levels.

🧠 The two fundamentals to lock in

1 byte = 8 bits.   An unsigned byte stores 0–255 (that's 2⁸ = 256 distinct values, counting from 0). These two facts power every memory-size question on the paper.

M

Interactive: the memory-size calculator

This calculation recurs across past papers and there's no calculator in the exam — so understand the method, not the answer. Play with it, then practise the worked logic on paper.

8

Why two MB answers? Decimal MB divides by 1,000,000; binary MiB divides by 1,048,576 (1024×1024). Both appear in exams — quote the one your formula sheet/lecturer uses, and show the working either way for method marks.

The exam-ready recipe (memorise this, not the number)

pixels = W × H
bytes  = pixels × channels × (bits-per-channel ÷ 8)
MB    = bytes ÷ 1,048,576  (binary)  or ÷ 1,000,000 (decimal)

Worked, the slide's image: 12,166,656 px × 3 bytes = 36,499,968 bytes ≈ 34.8 MiB (÷1,048,576) ≈ 36.5 MB (÷1,000,000). Greyscale version is ⅓ of that.

RGB & greyscale formats

How the bytes are actually laid out — and why a colour image is exactly three times the size of its greyscale twin.

RGB — three interleaved channels HIGH-YIELD

Per pixel: one byte each for Red, Green, Blue, stored together:

[R G B][R G B][R G B] … → 3 bytes / pixel = 24 bits
  • Bits in a byte? → 8
  • Bits per colour pixel? → 24
  • A 12 MP colour image? → ≈ 34.8 MiB / 36.5 MB (see calculator)

Splitting an image into R, G, B gives three greyscale images — each shows how much of that primary is present per pixel. A clear blue sky is bright in the blue channel, dark in red.

Greyscale — one intensity channel

A single intensity value I per pixel, 1 byte each:

0 ≤ I(x,y) ≤ 255 // unsigned byte
  • 0 → black, 255 → white, values between → shades of grey.
  • Min/max of an unsigned byte? → 0 and 255.
  • ⅓ the storage of the RGB version (1 byte vs 3).

↑ the full 0→255 intensity ramp. Every greyscale pixel is just a point on this strip.

EASY-TO-CONFUSE

Colour depth vs resolution. Resolution = how many pixels (sharpness). Colour depth = how many bits per pixel (range of colours/greys). A huge image at 1-bit depth is still just black-and-white; a tiny image at 24-bit is full-colour.

Origin & coordinates — (x,y) vs (r,c)

A small detail that bites people in code and on the exam. The image origin is the top-left, but there are two conventions for addressing a pixel, and they swap order.

Hover/move over the grid →

origin O(0,0) is top-left · move the mouse over the grid

Cartesian — image as a picture

  • Origin O(0,0) at top-left. x runs right, y runs down.
  • Pixel address = (x, y) = (column, row).
  • Image size quoted as width × height, e.g. 4272 × 2848.

Matrix — image as numbers

  • Same origin top-left, but indexed (r, c) = (row, column).
  • Size quoted as Rows × Columns, e.g. 2848 × 4272 — the swap of the Cartesian size!
  • This is the convention in Python / OpenCV / NumPy — get it wrong and your x/y are transposed.
🧠 Mnemonic — "Rhubarb & Custard"

Rows before Columns in matrix form — R comes first, like Rhubarb before Custard. Cartesian (x,y) is the everyday "across-then-up/down"; matrix (r,c) flips to "down-row first." If a question gives 2848 × 4272, it's quoting matrix (R×C); the same image as W×H is 4272 × 2848.

Point processing

The simplest class of operation: the new value of a pixel depends only on its own old value — never its neighbours. That single idea unifies brightness, negative, contrast and thresholding.

The master equation HIGH-YIELD

For every pixel (x,y): I′(x,y) = F( I(x,y) )

F is a mapping function from old intensity to new intensity. Choose F and you choose the effect. Because F ignores neighbours, point processing is fully described by a single input→output graph — which is exactly the playground below.

Interactive: the transfer-function playground

Pick an operation and watch two things: the graph of F (how every input grey 0–255 maps to an output) and a real intensity ramp transformed by it. Understanding the graph = understanding the operation.

Negative

I′ = 255 − I

Flips the graph to a downward diagonal. Black↔white, lights become darks. (The graph is a line of slope −1.)

Brightness

I′ = I + 50

Shifts the line up. Must clip to [0,255] — values above 255 stick at 255, below 0 stick at 0. That clipping is why the bright ramp's top "saturates" to flat white.

Threshold

I′ = 255 if I ≥ T
I′ = 0 otherwise

A step function → produces a binary image. The graph is flat-0 then jumps to flat-255 at T. T ∈ [0,255].

The clipping trap — almost always worth a mark

When adding brightness, a pixel at 230 + 50 = 280 cannot be stored in an unsigned byte. It is clipped to 255 (and negatives clip to 0). Forgetting clipping is the single most common point-processing error. State it explicitly: I′ = min(255, max(0, I+k)).

🧠 Hook — "Point = only the point"

If the new pixel needs to know a neighbour's value, it's not point processing — it's region processing (convolution, blur, edges; Week 2+). Point ops can be drawn as one input→output curve; region ops cannot.

A real application — segment by thresholding the right channel

The egg-box example: brown eggs are dark in the blue channel. Take the blue channel (a greyscale image 0–255), threshold it, and the eggs pop out as solid blobs separated from the carton. Lesson: choosing the right channel before a simple point op can do real segmentation work — you don't always need fancy methods.

OpenCV — the tool

The library you'll use for IP assignments. Conceptual familiarity is examinable even though deep API memorisation isn't.

What is it?

  • Open Source Computer Vision library (opencv.org).
  • Originally developed by Intel; open source.
  • Bindings: C/C++, Python, Android, iOS — but this course must use C/C++.
  • Gives high-level access (call threshold() rather than touch pixels) — but you can drop to per-pixel loops for operations that don't exist as a function.
  • An image is stored in a Mat (matrix).

The skeleton of every program

  • 1. Read an image (imread) — often as greyscale.
  • 2. Check it loaded (data == NULL → error & exit). Always.
  • 3. Process (a function call, or a per-pixel loop).
  • 4. Write out (imwrite) or display.

Note the (r, c) loop order — rows outer, columns inner — matching the matrix convention from §8.

The canonical threshold program — C++

#include <iostream>
#include "opencv2/core.hpp"
#include "opencv2/highgui/highgui.hpp"
#include "opencv2/imgproc.hpp"
using namespace std;
using namespace cv;

int main(int argc, char *argv[]) {
  Mat outputImage;
  // load as greyscale
  Mat inputImage = imread("harley.jpg", IMREAD_GRAYSCALE);
  if (inputImage.data == NULL) {                 // always check!
    cerr << "Error: Failed to open image file." << endl;
    return 1;
  }
  // threshold: above 128 -> 255, else 0 (binary)
  threshold(inputImage, outputImage, 128, 255, THRESH_BINARY);
  imwrite("harley_threshold.png", outputImage);
  return 0;
}

threshold() params: source, destination, threshold value (128), max value (255), type (THRESH_BINARY). THRESH_BINARY_INV just inverts the result. Bonus: THRESH_OTSU / THRESH_TRIANGLE pick the threshold for you (8-bit single-channel only).

Per-pixel access (region-of-interest threshold) — C++

for (int r = 0; r < inputImage.rows; r++)
  for (int c = 0; c < inputImage.cols; c++) {
    uchar& px = inputImage.at<uchar>(r, c);     // reference to pixel
    if (r < 30 || r > 280 || c < 165 || c > 360) { // outside ROI...
      if (px > 128) px = 255; else px = 0;          // ...threshold it
    }                                            // inside ROI: untouched
  }

Same idea in Python (cv2.imread, inputImage[r,c], inputImage.shape[0]/[1]) — but remember, Python isn't accepted for this course's marks.

Colour — perception & colour models

From your own Week 1 notes (Lecture 2). How humans see colour, why we need standardised colour spaces, the main models (RGB, YCrCb, HSV/IHS, Lab), and colour-based segmentation. Lower drill-priority than the core IP above, but classic short-answer fodder.

How we see colour

  • Newton (~1665): a prism splits white light into 7 colours (R O Y G B I V) → white light is a mixture.
  • Young → Helmholtz: three primaries (R, G, B) can approximate most colours → basis of modern displays.
  • Eye cells: rods (low-light, no colour) + cones (colour).
  • Three cone types: S (short→blue), M (medium→green), L (long→red).
  • Distribution: ≈65% red, ≈33% green, ≈2% blue (but blue cones are the most sensitive).
  • Absorption peaks: blue ≈445 nm, green ≈535 nm, red ≈575 nm.
SOURCE NUANCE

The cone peaks (445/535/575 nm) don't align with the later CIE 1931 standard primaries (B 435.8 / G 546.1 / R 700.0 nm). If asked, note this mismatch — standards are conventions, not the biology.

Additive vs subtractive mixing

  • Additive (screens): primaries R G B; all three combined → white.
  • Subtractive (paint/print): primaries C M Y; all combined → black.
  • Why a leaf is green: white sunlight hits it, pigments absorb everything except green, green is reflected, your green cones fire.

Why standardise? CIE & the chromaticity diagram

The problem: a colour looks right on the monitor but wrong when printed — different devices interpret colour differently. We need a device-independent reference.

  • CIE = Commission Internationale de l'Éclairage. Defined the 1931 XYZ colour space — the first standard, built from the best human-perception data of the time.
  • Colours represented by tristimulus X, Y, Z; normalise to chromaticity:
x = X/(X+Y+Z) y = Y/(X+Y+Z) z = Z/(X+Y+Z) → x + y + z = 1

So only x and y are needed (z is implied) → a 2D plot: the chromaticity diagram.

Boundary = pure, fully-saturated colours. Centre = white / desaturated. The triangle = a device's gamut (the colours it can actually reproduce). No standard gamut covers everything the eye sees.

RGB cube & YCrCb

  • RGB is a 3D cube: black (0,0,0) at one corner, white (255,255,255) at the opposite. Greys lie on the diagonal between them (R=G=B).
  • YCrCb separates brightness (Y / luma) from colour difference (Cb = blue-diff, Cr = red-diff) — used in broadcast/video.
Y  = 0.299R + 0.587G + 0.114B Cb = −0.1687R − 0.3313G + 0.5B + 128 Cr = 0.5R − 0.4187G − 0.0813B + 128

Compression win: the eye is poor at colour detail, so store one Cb & one Cr per four Y pixels (chroma subsampling). Notice Y's weights ≈ how a colour→grey conversion works (green dominates).

Perceptual spaces — HSV / IHS & Lab

Why: equal RGB distances ≠ equal perceived colour change. Perceptual spaces separate brightness from chromaticity, which is friendlier for measurement & human description.

  • IHS / HSV: Intensity/Value (brightness), Hue (the colour, an angle 0–359°), Saturation (purity; low S = washed out with white).
  • Lab (CIELAB): device-independent (Photoshop). L* lightness 0→100; a* green↔red; b* blue↔yellow.
I = (R+G+B)/3 S = 1 − [3/(R+G+B)]·min(R,G,B) H = θ if B≤G, else 360−θ

Colour-based segmentation with HSV — "find the yellow balloons" classic Q

Idea: hue isolates a colour regardless of how bright or washed-out it is — far more robust than thresholding RGB. To keep yellow, keep pixels whose hue is near yellow and discard the rest (a hue mask).

  • Yellow hue ≈ 60°, i.e. 1/6 of the wheel.
  • OpenCV often scales H to 0–255, so 60° → (1/6)×255 ≈ 42.
  • So pure yellow ≈ (H,S,V) = (60°,100%,100%) = (42,255,255).
  • Mask: keep where 22 ≤ H ≤ 62 → set 255 (keep) else 0 (discard).
SOURCE NUANCE

Hue's numeric value depends on the scale: degrees (0–359), 0–255 (OpenCV 8-bit), or 0–179 (OpenCV's actual default, halving degrees). Your notes use the 0–255 convention → yellow ≈ 42. State your scale in the answer.

Colour → greyscale: different programs use different weighted sums of R,G,B (OpenCV vs GIMP vs plain averaging) — so the "same" greyscale can differ between tools. Good example of "there isn't one right answer; conventions vary."

Write the general point-processing equation and give the formula for an image negative and for increasing brightness by 50. Note any pitfall.

3
Why is recognising printed text easier than cursive handwriting? Reference a technique.
Printed text is consistent and regular, so each character can be recognised by comparison with a prototype/template (template matching). Cursive is too irregular for templates, and its individual strokes can't be reliably isolated/segmented — so the problem remains essentially unsolved. (Hand-printed sits between: use structural stroke features.)
▼ reveal
3
Why use HSV rather than RGB to segment all the yellow objects in a scene? What hue value is yellow on a 0–255 scale?
In HSV, hue identifies the colour independently of brightness and saturation, so yellows of different shades/lighting share a similar H — a simple hue mask isolates them, which raw RGB thresholds can't do robustly. Yellow ≈ 60°; on a 0–255 scale that's (1/6)×255 ≈ 42, so keep e.g. 22 ≤ H ≤ 62.
▼ reveal
2
Distinguish image resolution from colour depth.
Resolution = the number of pixels (width × height) — controls spatial detail/sharpness. Colour depth = the number of bits per pixel — controls how many distinct colours/grey levels (2^bits). They're independent.
▼ reveal
2
In additive vs subtractive colour mixing, what are the primaries and what do "all combined" give?
Additive (light/screens): R, G, B; all combined → white. Subtractive (pigment/print): C, M, Y; all combined → black.
